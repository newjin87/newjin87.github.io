### 2. [Why the Gold Price Is Falling During the Iran War—and What it Means](https://www.newsweek.com/gold-price-iran-war-dollar-investments-oil-11722026)
**Source**: Newsweek | **Date**: 2026-03-23T15:46:00+00:00

> Gold shines when the world turns dangerous. At least, that’s what the gold bugs like everyone to think.
> Yet during the Iran war, it has done the opposite, falling sharply, even as oil prices jump and headlines scream of global crisis.
> At the time of writing, the price of gold is down by around 20 percent since February 28, the day the Iran war kicked off.
> Does this signal something much more ominous beneath the bonnet of the global economy, if even gold is falling at a time of crisis?
> The move feels backward, but only if you treat gold as a simple fear gauge.
> In most people’s minds, fear means gold. But in modern financial markets, the first place investors often run during a shock is the U.S. dollar.
> The dollar is the world’s most liquid currency, and the one used to price essentials like oil, global shipping, and insurance.
> Gold is priced in dollars. So when the dollar strengthens, gold becomes more expensive for buyers using pounds, euros, yen, or rupees. That usually reduces demand and pushes the gold price lower.
> This helps explain why the Iran war has produced what appears to be a paradox. The fear is real, but it’s showing up in a stronger dollar and higher bond yields, not a surge in gold.
> Gold’s decline since the conflict began reflects investors prioritizing cash‑like safety and easy access to dollars.
> That pattern has appeared in past crises, too, when gold initially fell as markets rushed to hold dollars instead.
> Gold doesn’t pay interest. That simple fact becomes important when bond yields rise.
> The Iran war has sent oil prices higher and revived fears that inflation will last longer than expected. When inflation looks stubborn, central banks are more likely to keep interest rates higher for longer, or at least delay cutting them.
> Bad news for gold. Higher interest rates make Treasury bonds and even cash more appealing compared with a metal that pays no income.
> This is the key misunderstanding behind the popular belief that inflation automatically boosts gold prices.
> In reality, gold often tracks real yields—interest rates after adjusting for inflation—more closely than it tracks inflation headlines on their own.
> If markets believe inflation will force central banks to stay tough on rates, real yields can rise, and that tends to push gold prices down.
> The Iran war is feeding inflation fears mainly through its impact on oil prices. That has pushed yields higher and led traders to scale back expectations for rate cuts, creating a headwind for a non‑yielding asset like gold.
> Gold is also one of the most liquid assets in the world, which sounds like a strength— until markets come under stress.
> During sharp sell‑offs, investors often sell their most liquid and profitable holdings to raise cash, cover losses elsewhere, or meet margin calls. That can pull gold prices down even if the long‑term case for owning it hasn’t changed.
> This phase is known as a classic “liquidity flush”.
> Gold’s recent slide has been driven partly by forced selling, as investors scrambled for quick cash to offset war‑related losses elsewhere in their portfolios.
> Stocks and other commodities have taken heavy hits, and gold, after a strong run, had plenty of profits available to be taken.
> The gold price most people see on their screens is heavily influenced by paper gold, such as futures contracts and exchange‑traded funds. In these markets, leverage and risk rules can force selling very quickly.
> When gold breaks key technical levels, automated trading algorithms can amplify the move, while risk managers tell funds to cut exposure.
> So the war isn’t just creating fear, it’s causing volatility.
> And volatility triggers mechanical selling in modern markets, even in assets that are supposed to be safe.
> There’s another unglamorous reason gold is falling: It has become crowded.
> Gold had already staged a historic run into early 2026. When a trade gets that popular, reversals can be sharp.
> In this kind of environment, a shock doesn’t always trigger new buying. Sometimes it pushes investors to lock in gains.
> The evidence is visible in exchange‑traded funds (ETFs).
> One widely cited data point this month has been large withdrawals from major gold ETFs.
> Reports have tracked heavy single‑day and multi‑week outflows from SPDR Gold Shares (GLD), with billions of dollars leaving in a short period.
> That kind of selling pressure can overwhelm safe‑haven instincts in the short term.
> Even if physical demand remains steady, ETF redemptions can push prices down because these products are a key route for fast‑moving capital.
> At the same time, analysts have pointed to straightforward profit‑taking after a meteoric rally.
> Gold’s recent rise attracted speculators as well as long‑term hedgers.
> Some holders were therefore less focused on protection and more on momentum, and momentum players tend to sell quickly when the trend breaks.
> Does gold falling during the Iran war guarantee a recession? Not by itself.
> What it more clearly signals is tightening financial conditions. A stronger dollar, rising yields, and investors scrambling for liquidity are all in play.
> That mix can raise the risk of a policy mistake or a market break somewhere else, especially if high oil prices keep inflation elevated and delay rate cuts.
> The takeaway is simple: Gold isn’t ignoring Iran, it’s reacting to the war’s economic consequences, which, right now, favor the dollar and higher interest rates.

> Market analysis by Vikram Subburaj, CEO, Giottus.com
> As of March 23, Bitcoin was trading around $68,000-$68,200 after a volatile week in which it had briefly traded above $74,000. In the near term, the market appears to be trading within a narrow tactical band. $68,000 remains the first support to watch, while the $70,000-$72,000 zone is the first meaningful resistance area. That matters because Bitcoin is no longer trading solely on crypto-native sentiment. It is reacting almost tick-for-tick to a broader global repricing of risk.
> On-chain data offers a more nuanced picture than price action alone. Glassnode data indicates that the $72K–$82 K band is a low-supply zone. So, if Bitcoin reclaims it, overhead resistance may be limited even if current prices are below that range.
> At the same time, its Percent Supply in Profit had recovered only to around 60%. This is an improvement from stress conditions but still below the 75%+ zone that would more convincingly signal a durable bull-market reset.
> The ETF tape also argues for caution rather than euphoria. On March 20, U.S. spot Bitcoin ETFs recorded a net outflow of about $52.1 million, with BlackRock’s IBIT accounting for the largest single-day withdrawal. March 21 and 22 were a weekend, so there were no regular U.S. ETF trading-session flow prints on those dates. Even so, the broader March picture had not completely broken down. Reports still described the month’s aggregate ETF flow trend as positive despite late-week softness. That is a useful distinction. One weak day is not the same as a regime change. But it does show that institutional money has become more selective at higher prices.
> The bigger driver on March 23 was macro. Reuters reported Brent crude at around $111.90 a barrel. This is up sharply amid the escalating Gulf conflict. Bond yields climbed and the dollar firmed as markets priced out aggressive rate cuts. That combination is not friendly to crypto. High oil prices raise inflation anxiety and inflation anxiety reduces the room for the Federal Reserve to turn dovish. The Fed has already held rates at 3.5%-3.75% and signaled only one cut in 2026. This reinforces the higher-for-longer backdrop. For crypto, that means liquidity remains constrained and rallies must work harder to sustain themselves.
> Among large-cap altcoins, the picture was softer rather than exuberant on the March 23 snapshot. Desks showed Ethereum near $2,056.82, down 1.36% in 24 hours; BNB around $627.53, down 0.61%; XRP at about $1.39, down 1.64%; and Solana near $86.46, down 1.05%. For TRON, there was a daily gain of roughly 2%-3%, depending on the currency pair snapshot. The broad takeaway is that altcoins were not staging a decisive independent breakout; they were mostly echoing Bitcoin’s macro-led hesitation.
> Bitcoin remains structurally better placed than it looked a few weeks ago. However, this is a market still being shaped more by oil, rates, and geopolitics than by internal crypto momentum. Unless Bitcoin reclaims $70,000-$72,000 with stronger participation and steadier ETF demand, traders should treat the current bounce as a test of resilience.

When tensions rise in the Middle East or other key energy corridors, uncertainty increases and traders react quickly. This is where the concept of an oil prices forecast becomes especially important, as analysts attempt to anticipate how disruptions might impact supply and demand.

> In 2026, these dynamics remain highly relevant. Ongoing tensions are keeping markets on edge, and even minor developments can lead to noticeable price swings.
> Supply Constraints and Production Decisions
> Beyond immediate geopolitical risks, oil prices are also shaped by supply decisions made by major producers. Organizations such as OPEC and its allies continue to play a central role in managing output levels.
> When global tensions rise, producers may adjust supply strategically. In some cases, they may increase production to stabilize markets and prevent extreme price spikes. In other situations, they may maintain or even reduce output to support higher prices.
> In recent years, production discipline has been a defining trend. Many oil producing countries are focused on maximizing revenue while avoiding oversupply. If geopolitical risks continue into 2026, this cautious approach could tighten supply further, supporting elevated price levels.

Shale production can respond relatively quickly to price changes, but it still faces constraints related to costs, regulation, and investor expectations. This creates a complex balance between global supply sources.

> While supply disruptions often dominate headlines, demand is equally important in determining oil prices. In 2026, global demand is influenced by several competing factors.
> Economic growth remains a key driver. If major economies such as the United States, China, and the European Union continue to expand, energy consumption is likely to increase. Industrial activity, transportation, and manufacturing all contribute to higher oil demand.

The transition toward renewable energy, electric vehicles, and improved energy efficiency is gradually reducing dependence on fossil fuels. Governments around the world are investing in cleaner technologies, which may slow the long term growth of oil consumption.

As inflationary pressures mounted in 2022 and 2023, the beef demand index turned lower, falling to 123 in 2022 and 120 in 2023. > The LMIC examines several components when calculating the retail all-fresh beef demand index. One component underlying the beef demand index is per capita beef consumption, which is calculated by dividing total beef disappearance by population. Total beef disappearance is calculated as total beef supplies minus beef exports and beef ending stocks. Total beef supplies are a function of domestic commercial beef production plus beef imports and beginning beef stocks.
> Domestic commercial beef production and beef imports are the driving factors underlying per capita consumption and the beef demand index. Since 2014, imports as a percentage of total beef supplies have ranged from 10% to 17%. Beef imports as a percentage of total beef supplies posting double digits also occurred from 1999 to 2007, when they ranged from 10% to 13%. Outside of those periods, the percentage was below 10%.

The larger percentage of total beef supplies coming from beef imports is a signal that domestic demand for ground beef products remains strong.
